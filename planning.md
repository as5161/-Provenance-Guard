# Provenance Guard — Planning

## Overview

Provenance Guard is a backend system that creative platforms can integrate to
assess whether submitted text was written by a human or generated by AI. It
accepts text through an API and analyzes it with two independent detection
signals: a Groq LLM that evaluates the content semantically, and a stylometric
analyzer that measures structural properties of the writing. The two signals are
combined into a single confidence score, which maps to one of three plain-language
transparency labels. Because wrongly labeling a human's work as AI-generated is
the more harmful error, the system expresses uncertainty honestly rather than
forcing a binary verdict, and gives creators a path to appeal. Every decision and
appeal is recorded in a structured audit log.

A submission enters at POST /submit, receives a unique content_id, and is sent to
both detection signals in parallel. Their scores are combined into one confidence
value, which selects a transparency label; the result is logged and returned. An
appeal enters at POST /appeal, looks up the original record by content_id, flips
its status to "under review," logs the appeal beside the original decision, and
returns a confirmation.

## Detection Signals

**Signal 1 — Groq LLM (semantic).** Sends the text to a Groq model and asks it to
judge whether the writing reads as human or AI-generated. Captures meaning, tone,
and stylistic coherence holistically. Output: a score from 0 (human) to 1 (AI).
Blind spot: it is an informed opinion, not a calibrated instrument — it can be
confidently wrong, is fooled by lightly-edited AI text, and may unfairly flag
humans who write in a polished, formal style.

**Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (structural).** Pure-Python math on the text.
Metrics: sentence-length variance and type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity).
Captures statistical uniformity — AI writing tends to be more regular; human
writing more variable. Output: a score from 0 (human) to 1 (AI). Blind spot: it
ignores meaning entirely, so deliberately uniform human writing (legal briefs,
academic abstracts) or a poem built on repeated structure can read as "AI" to it.

These two signals are independent: one is semantic, one is structural. Their
disagreement is itself informative — it tells the system to be less confident.

## Uncertainty & Confidence Scoring

The combined score is a weighted average of the two signals. If the two signals
disagree by more than 0.4, the result is pulled toward 0.5. This protects against
false positives: when one signal is confident the text is AI and the other reads
it as human, the system becomes less certain rather than splitting the difference
into a confident verdict.

On the 0–1 combined score:

| Score range   | Category       |
|---------------|----------------|
| >= 0.65       | likely AI      |
| 0.45 – 0.64   | uncertain      |
| < 0.45        | likely human   |

A score of 0.5 means the signals give no clear lean either way — genuine
uncertainty, shown to the user as such rather than a coin-flip verdict.

## Transparency Labels

**High-confidence AI:**
> "This content shows strong signals of AI generation. Our system assessed it with
> high confidence, but detection is not perfect — you are welcome to appeal with
> factual evidence."

**High-confidence human:**
> "This content reads as human-written. Our system found no strong signals of AI
> generation."

**Uncertain:**
> "Our system could not determine with confidence whether this was written by a
> human or AI. Treat this result as inconclusive."

## Appeals Workflow

Who can appeal: the creator of any classified submission.
What they provide: the content_id and their written reasoning (creator_reasoning).
What the system does: looks up the record by content_id, sets status to
"under_review," writes an appeal entry to the audit log beside the original
decision, and returns a confirmation. A human reviewer opening the queue would see
the original text, both signal scores, the verdict, and the creator's reasoning
side by side. Automated re-classification is out of scope.

## Edge Cases

1. **Structured poetry.** A poem with a repeated refrain has low sentence-length
   variance, so the stylometric signal may flag it as AI. Groq, reading it as
   intentional craft, disagrees — and because the design pulls disagreeing signals
   toward 0.5, confidence lands in the "uncertain" band, which is the safe outcome.

2. **Formal human nonfiction.** A legal or academic passage is naturally uniform
   and measured, so the stylometric signal may read it as AI. The same
   disagreement-lowers-confidence logic applies: Groq recognizes coherent human
   argument, the two signals diverge, and the result is pulled toward "uncertain"
   rather than a false "likely AI" verdict.

## Stretch Feature: Analytics Dashboard

A simple analytics view (GET /dashboard) showing detection patterns across all
submissions. Metrics displayed:
- Total submissions and a breakdown by attribution (likely_ai / uncertain / likely_human)
- Appeal rate (appeals filed ÷ total submissions)
- Average confidence score across all submissions (the additional metric of choice)

The dashboard reads from the same SQLite database using SELECT/COUNT queries and
returns the metrics as JSON, giving a platform operator a quick read on how the
detector is behaving and how often creators contest its verdicts.
